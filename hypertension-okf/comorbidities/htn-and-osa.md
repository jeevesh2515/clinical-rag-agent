# Hypertension in Obstructive Sleep Apnea

## Epidemiology

- ~50% of patients with HTN have undiagnosed OSA
- ~75% of patients with resistant HTN have OSA
- OSA is the most common identifiable cause of secondary HTN

## Screening

### Who to Screen
- Resistant HTN (≥3 agents including diuretic)
- Nocturnal non-dipping / reverse dipping on ABPM
- Symptoms: snoring, witnessed apneas, daytime somnolence (Epworth ≥10)
- Obesity (BMI ≥30), large neck circumference (≥40 cm / 16 inches)

### Screening Tool
**STOP-BANG questionnaire** (≥3 points → high risk):
- Snoring
- Tiredness during daytime
- Observed apnea
- Pressure (high BP)
- BMI >35
- Age >50
- Neck circumference >40 cm
- Gender male

## Treatment

### CPAP (First-Line for OSA)
- Modest BP reduction (2–5 mmHg average)
- Greater effect in patients with:
  - Resistant HTN
  - Severe OSA (AHI ≥30)
  - Good CPAP adherence (≥4 hours/night)
  - Daytime sleepiness

### Antihypertensive Selection
- No specific drug class is contraindicated in OSA
- ACEi/ARB may modestly reduce OSA severity via fluid shift reduction
- Avoid significant evening dosing (may worsen nocturnal hypoxia if already non-dipping)

## When to Refer

| Criteria | Referral |
|----------|----------|
| STOP-BANG ≥3 | Sleep study (polysomnography) |
| Resistant HTN + OSA symptoms | Sleep study + HTN specialist |
| Known OSA with uncontrolled BP | Increase CPAP pressure, HTN specialist |
| CPAP intolerant | ENT (for surgical options) |
