# Secondary HTN Red Flags

## When to Suspect Secondary Hypertension

### Clinical Features
- Age at onset <30 years (especially without family history or obesity)
- Sudden onset or rapid worsening of previously controlled HTN
- Resistant hypertension (not controlled on ≥3 agents including a diuretic)
- Malignant/accelerated hypertension (BP ≥180/120 with target organ damage)
- Severe HTN (Grade 3 / Stage 2)

### Common Secondary Causes by Feature

| Feature | Suspect | Initial Test |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| Hypokalemia + alkalosis | Primary hyperaldosteronism | Aldosterone:renin ratio |
| Paroxysmal HTN + headache/palpitations/sweating | Pheochromocytoma | Plasma metanephrines |
| Abdominal bruit, young onset, renal asymmetry | Renovascular HTN (renal artery stenosis) | Renal artery duplex/CTA/MRA |
| Truncal obesity, striae, moon facies | Cushing syndrome | Dexamethasone suppression test |
| Snoring, daytime somnolence | Obstructive sleep apnea | Sleep study (polysomnography) |
| Renal impairment, abnormal urinalysis | Renal parenchymal disease | Renal ultrasound, eGFR, urinalysis |
| Worsening HTN with ACEi | Bilateral renal artery stenosis | Renal imaging |

## Referral Criteria

| Criteria | Referral | Urgency |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Suspected secondary HTN (any cause) | Hypertension specialist or nephrology | Routine (2–4 weeks) |
| Accelerated/malignant HTN | Nephrology or emergency | Same day / emergency |
| Resistant HTN on ≥3 agents | Hypertension specialist | Routine |
| HTN in pregnancy | Obstetric medicine | Urgent |
| HTN + CKD stage 4+ | Nephrology | Routine–urgent |
