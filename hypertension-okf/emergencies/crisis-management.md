# Hypertensive Crisis Management

## Definition

- **BP ≥180/120 mmHg** (severe elevation)
- May or may not have acute target organ damage

## Initial Assessment

1. Confirm with repeat measurement in contralateral arm after 5 min rest
2. Targeted history: symptoms (chest pain, dyspnea, headache, vision change, neurologic deficits)
3. Targeted exam: fundoscopy, cardiac auscultation, lung auscultation, neurologic exam, pulses
4. Immediate labs: CBC, BMP (creatinine, K+, Na+), troponin, urinalysis, ECG
5. If neurologic symptoms: non-contrast head CT

## Management by Type

See [[emergencies/urgency-vs-emergency]] for full drug table.

### Hypertensive Emergency (admit to ICU)
- IV antihypertensive therapy
- Goal: ↓ MAP by 25% over **1 hour** (no faster — rapid drop can cause ischemia)
- If stable after initial reduction, continue oral therapy transition
- Exception: aortic dissection — target SBP <120 and HR <60 within 20 min

### Hypertensive Urgency (outpatient management)
- Restart or up-titrate oral medications
- Do NOT use rapid-acting sublingual nifedipine (contraindicated)
- Arrange follow-up within 24–48 hours

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Consequence | Correct Approach |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Rapid BP lowering in emergency | Cerebral hypoperfusion, stroke | ↓ 25% max in 1 hour |
| Sublingual nifedipine | Uncontrolled hypotension, reflex tachycardia, MI | Avoid entirely |
| Sending urgency home without follow-up | Return to ED with crisis | Recheck in 24–48h |
| Missing aortic dissection (chest/back pain + BP differential) | Fatal if missed | CT angiogram immediately |
